## Premise

This playbook synthesizes content from 10 domain experts across 3 source types — YouTube transcripts, LinkedIn posts, and blog/newsletter articles — into an operational guide for AI-powered SEO content production in 2026.

One finding anchors everything else: AI search and traditional SEO are not separate games. Every URL appearing in ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity responses comes from a live search index — Google or Bing — via Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). Your AI search visibility is structurally dependent on your organic search visibility. There is no shortcut around this.

> "Every single URL you see in an LLM output comes from a search engine API (Google or Bing)." — (source: Britney Muller, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7449472539904458752/, April 2026)

> "There is a strong correlation between top-performing citations in AI search and top-ranking URLs in search results. Because that's literally how RAG works." — (source: Lily Ray, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7450920000544055297/, April 2026)

## Part 1 — Foundation: Topical Authority Before AI Tools {#part-1}

### 1.1 Build Topic Ownership, Not Keyword Coverage

Before reaching for any AI content tool, the content architecture must be right. Topical authority — owning a complete conceptual map of a subject rather than targeting individual keywords — is the structural prerequisite for both Google rankings and AI citation.

Koray Tuğberk GÜBÜR's Topical Authority methodology defines this as: publishing content that covers all meaningful sub-topics, entities, and user intents within a domain — structured so search engines recognize your site as the authoritative source for that entire topic cluster, not just individual pages.

> "Discusses how AI is fundamentally changing search optimization and the transition from traditional SEO to AI-driven search optimization. Explains how large language models interpret content and the importance of semantic clarity for AI-based search engines." — (source: Koray Tuğberk GÜBÜR, https://medium.com/@ktgubur/learn-advanced-true-seo-with-topical-authority-2-0-a985869b46bf, 2025)

Why this matters for AI specifically: Kevin Indig's analysis of 21,482 ChatGPT citation rows found that the top 30 domains own 67% of all AI citations within any given topic. The top 10 domains take 46%. There are effectively ~30 seats at the citation table per topic.

> "Effectively, there are ~30 seats (domains) at the citation table for any given topic. Everything else is nearly invisible." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.growth-memo.com/p/the-science-of-how-ai-picks-its-sources, 23.03.2026)

**Action:** Before producing content with AI tools, audit your content architecture for topic gaps. Map all entities, relationships, and sub-intents in your target topic. Identify which clusters you own, which are partial, and which are missing entirely.

### 1.2 The Ranking-Citation Link

Pages ranking #1 in Google are cited by ChatGPT at 43.2% — 3.5x higher than pages ranking outside the top 20.

> "Among pages ranking #1 in Google, 43.2% were cited by ChatGPT. That was 3.5x higher than the citation rate for pages ranking beyond Google's top 20 results. Ranking well helps, but it doesn't guarantee citations." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.growth-memo.com/p/the-science-of-how-ai-picks-its-sources, 23.03.2026)

Optimizing for AI citation without first optimizing for Google rankings is like trying to get onto a shortlist for a job you haven't applied to.

## Part 2 — Content Architecture for AI Visibility {#part-2}

### 2.1 Structure Content for AI Retrieval

**Content length:** The 5K–10K word range produces the largest citation jump — nearly 2x more citations than shorter content. Pages above 20,000 characters average 10.18 citations each vs. 2.39 for pages under 500 characters. Note: this is vertical-specific. Finance content peaks at 5K–10K words then drops; SaaS shows the weakest length effect.

> "The 5K-to-10K jump is the largest single step — nearly 2x. Pages above 20,000 characters average 10.18 citations each vs. 2.39 for pages under 500 characters." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.growth-memo.com/p/the-science-of-how-ai-picks-its-sources, 23.03.2026)

**Front-loading:** The bottom 10% of any page earns only 2.4–4.4% of citations. The 10–20% band is where AI reads hardest. Put your most citable claims, data, and direct answers in the first 30% of the page.

> "Put your most citable claims and data in the first 30% of the page — no matter what industry you're in. Summaries and conclusions rarely get cited." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.growth-memo.com/p/the-science-of-how-ai-picks-its-sources, 23.03.2026)

**Heading structure signals:** An analysis of 6.8 million subheadings found a measurable correlation between specific H2–H4 structures and ChatGPT citation rates.

> "An analysis of 6.8 million subheadings reveals a measurable correlation between specific heading structures and ChatGPT citation rates. Subheading formatting appears to act as a significant sorting signal for AI retrieval systems." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7450521258842185728/, April 2026)

**Citation breadth over depth:** A single evergreen page covering 10+ query intents outperforms 10 single-intent pages. Build category-level pages that answer "what is it", "who uses it", "how to choose", and "pricing" in one URL.

### 2.2 Brand Visibility Gap in AI Responses

A significant underreported problem: most AI citations don't name your brand even when they cite your content.

> "Around 62% of brand citations fly under the radar. The domain gets a source link, but the brand name is left out of the actual answer text. Only about 13% of citations explicitly name the brand." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7451974262686412800/, April 2026)

**Action:** Audit your brand's AI citation visibility separately from link citation. Content that states your brand name explicitly in the context of answering a question drives higher brand mention rates in AI responses.

## Part 3 — AI-Powered Content Production Workflow {#part-3}

### 3.1 AI Content Quality Has Crossed the Threshold

> "The common belief is that AI-generated blog posts are inherently low-quality compared to human-made content... AI-generated content has improved significantly, and is no longer necessarily inferior to human-written content." — (source: Ryan Law, https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-content-wasnt-good-enough-now-it-is/, 16.03.2026)

Nathan Gotch's case study of 487 Google search results: 83% of top-ranking results use human-generated content, but Google does not explicitly penalize AI authorship. The penalty applies to thin, unhelpful content regardless of who or what produced it.

> "Google does not have an explicit policy penalizing AI-generated content itself. Instead, Google applies manual actions against websites using spammy, low-quality AI-generated content that violates their guidelines. Google's stance is not anti-AI, but rather anti-spam." — (source: Nathan Gotch, https://www.gotchseo.com/does-google-penalize-ai-content/, 15.11.2025)

### 3.2 The Production Workflow

**Step 1 — Content Gap Analysis**
Use Ahrefs or Semrush to identify topics competitors rank for that you don't. Feed output to Claude for clustering by topical intent.

**Step 2 — Research from Conversation Data**
Pull from sales conversation transcripts, support tickets, and live chat logs — the actual language customers use. This surfaces prompts that keyword tools never surface.

> "My number one tip: how do you figure out what people might be prompting? Look for conversations that actually happen between your target audience and the company. My favorite are sales conversations. I upload them to an LLM and ask traditional marketing and product questions." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RwKKLnyXCig, 21.01.2026)

> "Prompts are on average five times longer than keywords in traditional SEO." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RwKKLnyXCig, 21.01.2026)

**Step 3 — Semantic Brief Building**
Structure briefs around entity relationships and context graphs, not keyword lists.

> "Created 'Koray's Agents' — 48 AI agents for semantic SEO professionals, covering linguistic analysis, semantic extraction, entity mapping, and SEO auditing." — (source: Koray Tuğberk GÜBÜR, https://medium.com/@ktgubur/korays-agents-generative-ai-agents-for-semantic-seo-and-topical-authority-d4b247fac72a, 2025)

**Step 4 — Draft Generation with Human Review**
AI for first draft, 30–45 minutes human editorial review per 1,500 words. Inject original data, first-hand observations, and examples AI cannot generate.

> "Full AI content creation system: from content gap analysis and keyword prioritisation through to internal linking, screenshot generation and formatting for publication. Fully customisable." — (source: Ryan Law, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7447967518163353601/, April 2026)

**Step 5 — Content Optimization Tools**

> "Based on price-to-value ratio analysis, Rankability, Surfer, and Clearscope stand out as the top choices. Rankability ($149/month). Surfer SEO ($99/month). Clearscope ($189/month)." — (source: Nathan Gotch, https://www.rankability.com/blog/best-ai-seo-tools/, 10.01.2026)

## Part 4 — GEO: Extending Visibility Into AI Search {#part-4}

### 4.1 YouTube Is the Highest-Leverage GEO Signal

> "YouTube mentions are the strongest correlating factor followed by YouTube mention impressions. OpenAI has trained on over a million hours of YouTube transcripts. YouTube is the most cited domain in AI Mode and AI Overviews." — (source: Louise Linehan, Ahrefs Head of Research, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RwKKLnyXCig, 21.01.2026)

**Action:** Invest in YouTube content or sponsor relevant YouTube creators. Being mentioned in YouTube videos — even without owning a channel — correlates with higher AI citation rates.

### 4.2 Off-Site Brand Mentions Are Now Essential

> "Reddit, Quora, Facebook Groups, LinkedIn and other UGC sites are among the most heavily cited websites in AI search. It is essential for brands to be recommended and top-of-mind across these platforms." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/a-reflection-on-seo-and-ai-search, 20.01.2026)

Monitor competitor brand mentions in AI responses. Use Brand Radar to identify topics where competitors are mentioned and you are not.

> "One of the great use cases for brand radar: you can find topics that your competitors are being mentioned on that you're just not really being associated with." — (source: Glenn Allsopp, Ahrefs Head of Marketing Strategy, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RwKKLnyXCig, 21.01.2026)

### 4.3 Crystal Carter's GEO Framework

> "Optimizations for LLMs can deliver significant visibility improvements independent of Google rankings, noting a 15% uplift in LLM visibility achieved within a week." — (source: Crystal Carter, https://searchengineland.com/plan-for-geo-2026-evolve-search-strategy-463399, 10.01.2026)

## Part 5 — Quality Control and Anti-Patterns {#part-5}

### 5.1 Five GEO Tactics That Will Eventually Backfire

**1. Scaling AI content without editorial oversight**

> "For many sites, the content scaling appeared to work, until it didn't." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/your-geo-strategy-might-be-destroying, 18.03.2026)

**2. Self-promotional listicles at scale**

> "The blog contained 191 self-promotional listicles... [This company] dropped -49% in organic visibility between January 21 and February 2, 2026." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/is-google-finally-cracking-down-on, 03.02.2026)

**3. Artificial content freshening** — updating publish dates without substantively updating content.

**4. "Summarize with AI" buttons with hidden prompt injection** — Microsoft's security research (February 2026) classified this as a security threat and potential legal liability.

**5. Excessive comparison pages at scale** — one company created 51 comparison pages through 2025; blog traffic and ChatGPT citations both dropped starting late January 2026.

### 5.2 The AI Slop Loop

> "One AI-generated article hallucinates a detail → sites running AI content pipelines scrape and regurgitate it → more AI-generated sites scrape the same misinformation → enough citations exist for RAG-based systems to treat it as fact." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/the-ai-slop-loop, 15.04.2026)

Lily Ray published a fake Google core update on her blog in January 2026. Within 24 hours, Google AI Overviews was serving the fabricated information as fact. **Implication:** If your production workflow sources from AI summaries of industry news rather than primary sources, you are amplifying misinformation into your own content.

## Part 6 — Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Is GEO a genuinely new discipline or traditional SEO rediscovered?

**Britney Muller:**
> "Y'all are just rediscovering SEO by way of AI SEO or GEO. Everyone's acting like optimizing for LLMs is an entirely new discipline that we need to do ASAP. It's not." — (source: Britney Muller, https://x.com/BritneyMuller/status/1973121024494346407, February 2026)

**Crystal Carter:** GEO is a distinct discipline with its own frameworks, measurement systems, and optimization targets.
> "Crystal Carter focuses specifically on Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) as a distinct discipline from traditional SEO." — (source: Crystal Carter, https://searchengineland.com/crystal-carter-interview-smx-advanced-2025-456460, 01.06.2025)

**My position: Britney Muller is right about the foundation; Crystal Carter is right about the surface.**

The RAG pipeline makes this empirically clear: if you rank in Google, you get cited in AI. The core skills are unchanged. Practitioners calling traditional SEO tactics "GEO innovations" are doing what Lily Ray calls the "Bait and Switch" — repackaging structured data and optimized headings as novel AI search techniques.

However, Crystal Carter is correct that there are genuinely new measurement surfaces and distribution channels — YouTube citation signals, brand entity recognition, off-site UGC presence — that traditional SEO metrics don't capture.

The practical implication: spend 80% of your effort on proven SEO fundamentals, 20% on AI-specific measurement and distribution. Anyone inverting that ratio is selling you something.

---

### Disagreement 2: Scale AI content volume aggressively or prioritize depth?

**Nathan Gotch:** AI and agents should empower teams to deliver better work at greater scale.
> "AI and agents should empower your team to deliver better work. They shouldn't turn your agency into a founder bottleneck that can't scale." — (source: Nathan Gotch, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7449425459756568576/, April 2026)

**Lily Ray:** Scaling AI content volume follows the exact same pattern as every previous SEO shortcut — works for months, then crashes hard.
> "Rapid growth in SEO often leads to an equally rapid crash on the other side. The crash can be incredibly difficult to recover from, sometimes requiring multiple years of extremely hard work." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/your-geo-strategy-might-be-destroying, 18.03.2026)

**My position: Lily Ray's risk assessment is correct. Nathan Gotch's operational model is the right implementation given that risk.**

Scale the *production workflow*, not the *output volume*. Scaling AI-generated articles to fill keyword gaps = high risk with documented crash patterns. Scaling the workflow itself (AI-assisted research, briefing, drafting, internal linking) to produce higher-quality content faster = low risk, high upside. Every piece still needs original insight, editorial review, and a reason to exist beyond keyword coverage.

---

### Disagreement 3: How much should traffic still factor into SEO success measurement?

**Kevin Indig:** Traffic and pipeline have decoupled. Measurement must shift to brand lift and pipeline influence.
> "Traffic is either down for many sites... but demos and pipeline are up for brands that shifted from chasing clicks to building authority." — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.growth-memo.com/p/the-great-decoupling, 19.01.2026)

**Patrick Stox (implied from data):** Google still dominates traffic at a 190x advantage over ChatGPT. The decoupling narrative is premature.
> "ChatGPT Has 12% of Google's Search Volume but Google Sends 190x More Traffic." — (source: Patrick Stox, https://ahrefs.com/blog/chatgpt-has-12-percent-of-googles-search-volume/, 15.02.2026)

**My position: Both are right on different timescales, but Kevin Indig's framing is more useful for decision-making in 2026.**

Patrick Stox's data is correct — Google still dominates traffic in 2026. But the relevant question is not "where is traffic today" but "where is traffic trending and what does that mean for content investment now." This matches what I observed at terra-plat.vn: content ranking well on Google drove organic traffic, but the actual conversion signal came from brand search volume growth and direct demo requests — not traffic numbers themselves. Traffic was the proxy, not the outcome.

The practical move: maintain SEO rigor for traffic now, but start building brand lift and LLM visibility measurement infrastructure today, before those metrics become the primary success criteria.

## Part 7 — What I Rejected and Why

### Rejected Idea 1: Self-promotional listicles for AI search visibility

Multiple GEO practitioners recommend "best [category]" content ranking your own product at #1 as a quick path to AI search citations.

**Why I rejected it:** Lily Ray documented 7 specific companies losing 29–49% organic visibility starting 21.01.2026 — all heavy users of self-promotional listicles. The crash pattern matches every previous short-term SEO tactic. The tactic also fails the basic credibility test: a company ranking itself #1 without independent evidence is structurally untrustworthy. Tactics built on manipulating rather than earning visibility consistently fail at scale.

> "Producing 191 self-promotional listicle articles feels more like an intentional strategy than an accident. [This company] dropped -49% in organic visibility." — (source: Lily Ray, https://lilyraynyc.substack.com/p/is-google-finally-cracking-down-on, 03.02.2026)

---

### Rejected Idea 2: Treating "prompt volume" as a meaningful content planning metric

Multiple GEO tools sell prompt volume tracking — how many people search certain phrases in ChatGPT — as a primary input for AI SEO content strategy.

**Why I rejected it:**

> "Quick reminder for anyone doing AI SEO: 'Prompt volume' is fake." — (source: Nathan Gotch, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7447617685263159296/, April 2026)

LLM responses are non-deterministic and highly personalized. What tools report as "prompt volume" is directional sampling, not real aggregate data. Use traditional search volume data as the planning baseline, supplemented by fan-out query analysis for content gap identification.

## Part 8 — My Original Ideas

### Using Operational Data Asymmetry as a Content Moat for B2B Service Businesses

Kevin Indig's most actionable GEO advice is to pull research from sales conversation transcripts. The insight is correct — but it assumes enterprise-scale sales operations with formalized call recording. Most B2B SMEs don't have this.

At Vina Payroll/terra-plat.vn, I ran SEO for a payroll outsourcing service targeting Japanese and European FDI companies operating in Vietnam. We had no call recording infrastructure, no content team, and keyword tools gave us generic terms like "payroll services Vietnam" — terms that described our category but not the questions our actual clients were asking.

What we did have: every pre-sales email from a prospective client asking a question, every support ticket from an existing client confused about Vietnamese social insurance regulations, and every onboarding note where a client asked "but how does this actually work in practice?" This was raw material no competitor could replicate — because it came from our specific client base operating in our specific regulatory context. This approach contributed to 200%+ organic traffic growth over approximately 18 months.

**The idea:** For B2B service businesses without enterprise sales infrastructure, build what I call an **Operational Data Asymmetry** content strategy. Three components:

**Component 1 — Regulatory gap content.** In regulated industries (payroll, legal, accounting, compliance), regulations change faster than most competitors update their content. The gap between when a regulation changes and when the first quality explainer appears in search results is typically 2–8 weeks. Publishing within 48 hours consistently captures this window. This content serves AI citation particularly well because it answers factual questions that AI models frequently hallucinate on — creating a direct citation opportunity where the supply of accurate answers is genuinely scarce.

**Component 2 — Client confusion mining.** Export every support ticket and pre-sales inquiry into a spreadsheet. Group by theme. The most common confusion points are your highest-value content opportunities — because they represent real questions that real buyers in your exact market are asking. This is Kevin Indig's "conversation data" approach, sourced from operational systems every B2B company already has rather than sales call recordings they may not have.

**Component 3 — Practitioner specificity as a competitive moat.** AI search tends to cite authoritative sources for general topics but frequently fails on nuanced, market-specific implementation questions. A piece titled "How to calculate overtime pay under Vietnam's Labor Code 2024 for manufacturing shift workers" has essentially no competition from global sources — and it answers a specific question that a payroll manager at a foreign-invested enterprise in Ho Chi Minh City will type into both Google and ChatGPT. The specificity is the moat.

**Why this could work beyond my specific context:** The mechanism is generalizable. Any B2B service business operating in a regulatory environment that global AI models understand poorly has an asymmetric advantage: the questions are real, the answers are scarce, and the citation opportunity is disproportionately accessible. Most global AI SEO content focuses on US/UK English-language markets. B2B services in emerging markets — payroll in Southeast Asia, compliance in Eastern Europe, tax advisory in Latin America — face structurally lower competition for AI citation on the exact questions their buyers are actually asking.

The core insight from Kevin Indig applies here too:

> "Classic search keywords are not hitting the context that people are looking for. My number one tip is about how do you bridge that disconnect? How do you figure out what people might be prompting?" — (source: Kevin Indig, https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RwKKLnyXCig, 21.01.2026)

The answer, for businesses without sales transcripts, is already sitting in your support inbox.

## Part 9 — Weaknesses of This Playbook

### Weakness 1: The RAG assumption has an expiration date

The entire playbook rests on the premise that AI citations flow through RAG from search engine indices. If AI models develop direct web indexing that bypasses search results, the SEO-first foundation becomes obsolete. Marie Haynes is already tracking this transition:

> "Search is becoming agentic, task-oriented, and deeply integrated with AI that learns, improves, and even negotiates on our behalf." — (source: Marie Haynes, https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7450931719446155264/, April 2026)

Her research on Google's "Google-Agent" and Universal Commerce Protocol signals a future where AI agents transact directly without passing through search results at all. The playbook is valid for 2025–2026. By 2027–2028, agentic commerce may require significant revision.

### Weakness 2: Half-life of AI SEO advice is extremely short

Every recommendation is based on research collected in a narrow window (late 2025 – April 2026). The field is changing faster than any other SEO sub-topic. Tactics working today may stop working as AI systems become more sophisticated at detecting optimization patterns.

### Weakness 3: No direct experimentation

Every recommendation derives from expert content, not from direct A/B testing or controlled experiments. Results may not transfer across industries, company sizes, or content types.

### Weakness 4: Non-English market applicability is untested in AI search specifically

Most research is grounded in US/UK market data. AI Overviews penetration, RAG citation behavior, and content patterns may differ significantly in Vietnamese, Japanese, or multilingual search behavior. The original idea in Part 8 addresses this gap — but it remains hypothesis rather than validated AI search strategy specifically.

### Weakness 5: The content quality audit has sampling limitations

The Claude API audit validating this expert list was run on approximately 6,000 characters per expert — a fraction of each person's full body of work. The audit may systematically undervalue experts who produce less frequent but higher-impact research.

## Summary: The 5 Non-Negotiables

1. **Rank in Google first.** RAG pulls from search indices. AI citation follows organic rankings. There is no GEO shortcut that bypasses this.
2. **Front-load your claims.** The first 30% of a page gets cited disproportionately. Put the answer before the explanation.
3. **Own a topic cluster, not keywords.** ~30 domains own 67% of citations per topic. Be one of them.
4. **Scale the workflow, not the volume.** Use AI for speed and coverage. Use humans for quality and original insight.
5. **Measure brand lift and pipeline influence, not just traffic.** Traffic is the lagging proxy. The leading indicators are brand search growth and LLM mention rate.
